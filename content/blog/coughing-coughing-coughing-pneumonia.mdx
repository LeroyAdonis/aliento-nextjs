<p>Coughing can be a simple common cold with a “wet cough” or a “dry cough”, for which there are several cough mixtures available - the best being XXXX or XXXX which you can get over the counter at any pharmacy like XXXX or Dischem or Clicks.</p><h1>Acute coughing with fever temperature ≥ 37,5% Celsius, chest pain - requires a Chest X-ray.</h1><p>The diagnosis is most likely Pneumonia. There are 4 Classifications and causes of Pneumonia:</p><p>1.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Community-acquired Pneumonia.</p><p>This may be <strong>Primary</strong> - meaning it was acquired from someone else, while you were healthy or <strong>Secondary</strong>, if you have an underlying disease. There are at least 6 different bacteria responsible for the onset of Pneumonia and 3 different viruses. (Click here if you want to learn about them and what medication should be used to treat them).</p><p></p><img class="rounded-xl max-w-full h-auto my-6" src="https://1dp2ojinmz645uqk.public.blob.vercel-storage.com/blog/images/1774437414497-community-coughing-photo-1682545957221-5.avif" alt="community coughing_photo-1682545957221-5c1e9e74c73b.avif"><p>2.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; The second type of Pneumonia is called <strong>Nosocomial Pneumonia</strong>. This usually occurs about 48 hours ≥ admission to hospital. So, this means, it is a pneumonia that you acquired in the hospital. At least 6 types of bacteria are responsible for this type of pneumonia.</p><img class="rounded-xl max-w-full h-auto my-6" src="https://1dp2ojinmz645uqk.public.blob.vercel-storage.com/blog/images/1774437362153-hospital-1758654859923-339c0f2f925e.avif" alt="hospital-1758654859923-339c0f2f925e.avif"><p></p><p><strong>3.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Aspiration Pneumonia. </strong>Those with strokes,<strong> </strong>passed out due to drunkenness, unconsciousness or recovery after a seizure; oesophageal reflux, myasthenia diseases which causes weakness of nerves and rapid fatigue of muscles, motor neuron disease that causes weakness of cranial nerves (bulbar palsy), even poor dental hygiene - can cause aspiration pneumonia.</p><p><strong>4.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Immunocompromised Patients.</strong> Here Streptococcus Pneumoniae and Haemophylus Influenza, together with Staphylococcus Aureus, which are the most common bacteria in most of the pneumonias are most common here too.</p><p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p><h2>Clinical Features to look out for</h2><h3>Symptoms</h3><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Fever</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Rigor (shaking and shivering)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Malaise (general feeling of discomfort or unease)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Anorexia</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Dyspnoea (Uncomfortable, fast or slow breathing)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Cough</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Purulent sputum (pus-like looking sputum)</p><p></p><img class="rounded-xl max-w-full h-auto my-6" src="https://1dp2ojinmz645uqk.public.blob.vercel-storage.com/blog/images/1774437555854-sputum-photo-1765389247930-77aea9bfd7b4.avif" alt="sputum_photo-1765389247930-77aea9bfd7b4.avif"><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Haemoptysis (blood in the sputum)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Pleuritic Chest Pain (sharp, stabbing chest pain)</p><h3>Signs</h3><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Fever</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Cyanosis (bluish, greyish discoloration of the lips or tongue)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Confusion</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Tachypnoea (heavy fast breathing)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Tachycardia (very fast beating of the heart ≥ 99/100 beats per minute)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hypotension (BP ≤120/80)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Signs of consolidation in the lung (This is when the doctor listens to your lungs, these are the signs present) - your doctor may explain these to you.</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Diminished expansion</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Dull percussion notes</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Increases vocal fremitus/vocal resonance</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Bronchial breathing</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Pleural rub</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2>These are the tests your healthcare provider should do if Pneumonia is expected</h2><p>&nbsp;</p><p>1.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Sputum and Pleural Fluid test - this is called <strong>Cytology</strong></p><p>2.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Chest X-ray. Healthcare practitioners should look for opacities (areas of increased densities), hilar enlargements (swollen lymph nodes), consolidations (areas of thickness) , lung collapse, pleural (lung) effusions, bony secondaries (spots). Also peripheral lesions (outside of the lung area), superficial lymph nodes (which may have to be biopsied). <strong>Learn these terms, and ask your doctors about them.</strong></p><p></p><img class="rounded-xl max-w-full h-auto my-6" src="https://1dp2ojinmz645uqk.public.blob.vercel-storage.com/blog/images/1774437644852-x-ray.jpeg" alt="X-ray.jpeg"><p>3.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Blood Tests. Full Blood Count (FBC), Urea and Electrolytes (U&amp;E), Liver Function Tests (LFT’s), C-reactive Protein (CRP), arterial blood gasses, blood cultures and atypical cytology.</p><p>4.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Sputum is sent for microscopy and culture.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>These features indicate SEVERITY, take note</h3><p>&nbsp;</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Tachypnoea ≥ 30/min</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Diastolic BP &lt; 60mmHg</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Age ≥ 60 years</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Underlying disease</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Confusion</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Atrial Fibrillation</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Multilobar involvement</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Urea ≥ 7mmol/L</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Albumin &lt; 35 g/L</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hypoxia (low Oxygen) PaO2 ≤ 8kPa</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; White Blood Count ≤ 4000 x 109/L</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Bacteraemia</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>This is how a person with Pneumonia should be treated</h3><p>&nbsp;</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Oxygen should be maintained PaO2 ≥ 8Pa</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Antibiotics should definitely cover Streptococcus Pneumoniae&nbsp; (example Amoxicillin 250-500mg/8h orally and atypicals such as Erythromycin 500mg/12 orally)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Intravenous Fluids may be needed if the patient presents with anorexia, dehydration, shock; Analgesia - 1g/6hrs</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>The following conditions could result in Complications</h3><p>&nbsp;</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Pleural effusion</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Emphysema</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Lung abscesses</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Respiratory Failure</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Septicaemia</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Pericarditis (infection/inflammation of the heart covering)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Myocarditis (infection/inflammation of the heart muscle)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Cholestatic Jaundice (reduced bile flow causing jaundice - a yellow tinge over the skin and sclera of the eyes)</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h3>Here is what you can do to help prevent Pneumococcal Infection</h3><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Where possible, procure pneumococcal vaccine ( 23 valent Pneumovax II, 0,5mL sc) if you have:</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Chronic Heart or Lung Conditions</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Cirrhosis (late stage, permanent scarring of the liver due to chronic hepatitis or alcohol abuse)</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Nephrosis (damaged kidney diseases causing excessive excretion of protein)</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Diabetes Mellitus</p><p>o&nbsp;&nbsp; Immunosuppressive Diseases such as: HIV/AIDS, Splenectomy, On Chemotherapy</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Contraindications of the vaccine is: Pregnancy, lactation, fever</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; If high risk of fatal pneumococcal infection (asplenia, sickle-cell disease, nephrosis, post-transplant), revaccination after 6 years (3-5yrs in children .2 years old), unless they had a severe vaccine reaction.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><p>For Healthcare Providers click here if you need to refresh on empirical treatment of pneumonia.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><p>References</p><p>1.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;
