<p>Asthma affects at least 5-8% percent of the population. It is characterized by recurrent episodes of difficult of breathing , coughing and wheezing caused by reversible airway obstruction. There is a type of Asthma called a Cough-variant asthma (CVA), that is a sub-set of the usual asthma, where cough is the primary symptom, but the patient presents without wheezing, chest tightness or dyspnoea. This type of Asthma accounts for between 25% - 42% of cases, but gets so overlooked and mis-diagnosis due to poor recognition of the condition. CVA is a common cause of chronic cough, may present with less allergen-related  coughing, may have lower levels of eosinophilia and milder airway remodeling. Research has even shown that CVA can progress to the usual type of Asthma. Therefore, healthcare workers should be on the lookout for this type of Asthma.There are three factors that contribute to classic Asthma. This includes (via airway narrowing): bronchial muscle contraction, triggered by a variety of stimuli; mucosal swelling/inflammation caused by mast cell and basophil degranulation resulting in the release of inflammatory mediators; increased mucus production.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2>These are the Symptoms of Asthma</h2><p>&nbsp;</p><p>Asthmatic patients will often experience intermittent difficulty in breathing, wheeze often, cough profusely (often at night).</p><h3>These are the factors that often bring on the symptoms above:</h3><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Cold Air, emotion, allergens (house mites, dust, pollen, animal fur), infection, drugs ( usually aspirin, non-steroidal anti-inflammatories and Beta-blockers)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Diurnal variation ( daily cycles in environmental, biological, or physical processes that occur within a 24-hour cycle, typical driven by solar radiation and the Earth’s rotation)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Exercise: quantify their exercise tolerance</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Disturbed sleep: the more sleep is disturbed, the worse the Asthma is</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Acid Reflux: this is a known symptom of Asthma</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Other atopic Diseases (allergic diseases), example eczema, hay fever, allergy. Or known family history of atopy.</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; The home (especially the bedroom): Pets, carpets, duvets, feather pillows, floor cushions or other soft furnishings.</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Occupation: If symptoms remit during weekends or holidays, something at work may be a trigger. The way to figure this out is to measure your peak flow at work as well as at home during the same time.</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Days per week off per work or school</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2>Herewith the Signs of Asthma</h2><p>&nbsp;</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Fast breathing (tachypnoea)</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Audible wheezing</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hyperinflated Chest</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Hyperresonant percussion note - the healthcare worker will hear a loud low-pitched sound indicating excessive air in the chest, sounding louder and deeper than normal lung resonance</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Diminished air entry</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Widespread polyphonic wheeze - two independent melodic lines</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; <strong>Severe Attack: </strong>Inability to complete sentences, pulse ≥ 110bpm, respiratory rate more than 15/min, Perfusion Rate, 50% of predicted.</p><p>·&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; <strong>Life-threatening Attack: </strong>silent chest, cyanosis (turning blue around lips and tongue), bradycardia ≤ 60bpm, exhaustion, Perfusion Rate (PEFR) ≤ 33% of predicted, confusion, feeble respiratory effort.</p><p></p><p><strong>&nbsp;</strong></p><h3>These are the Tests that needs to be done for an Asthmatic patient</h3><p>&nbsp;</p><img class="rounded-xl max-w-full h-auto my-6" src="https://1dp2ojinmz645uqk.public.blob.vercel-storage.com/blog/images/1774430020443-img-5395.jpeg" alt="IMG_5395.jpeg"><p>·Spirometry ( a common, non-invasive lung function test that measures how much air you inhale, exhale and how quickly you can empty your lungs): for a positive Asthma test , (decrease FEV1/ Increase FVC), residual volume), Skin-prick tests may help identify allergens.
